Module 3 · Lesson 1 — Introduction to Microbial Genomes

Q1What are the informational macromolecules?
Answer
  • The informational macromolecules are nucleic acids (DNA and RNA) and proteins.
  • Anchor vocab: gene = functional unit of genetic information · genome = all genetic elements in a cell · DNA = the blueprint · RNA = transcription product.
Explanation
These are the molecules that store or transmit biological information. Everything else in the module is about how that information is packaged, copied, and expressed, so pin the vocabulary now: a gene is the functional unit, the genome is the full set of genetic elements in the cell, and the central players are the nucleic acids plus the proteins they encode.
Q2What are the basic properties of DNA?
Answer
  • Nucleic acids are polymers of nucleotides; each nucleotide = nitrogenous base + pentose sugar + phosphate group.
  • DNA is double-stranded, a double helix, runs antiparallel, with complementary base pairing (A–T, C–G).
  • Don't confuse with a nucleoside = base + sugar only (no phosphate).
  • RNA differs: ribose sugar, single-stranded, uracil replaces thymine; three types — mRNA, tRNA, rRNA.
Explanation
The properties matter because DNA's structure is what lets it be copied faithfully. Antiparallel strands held by complementary base pairing mean either strand can serve as a template for the other — the structural basis of replication you'll meet in L2. RNA is built on the same nucleotide logic but the differences (ribose, single-stranded, uracil) are exactly the distractor set the exam likes to test.
Flashcard
Nucleotide vs nucleoside — what's the difference?
Nucleotide = base + sugar + phosphate. Nucleoside = base + sugar only (no phosphate).
Flashcard
Name the three types of RNA and what each does.
mRNA carries the message · tRNA brings amino acids · rRNA builds the ribosome.
Q3What are the bonds within and between DNA strands?
Answer
  • Within a strand: phosphodiester bonds — the covalent sugar–phosphate backbone.
  • Between the two strands: hydrogen bonds between complementary bases.
  • A–T = 2 H-bonds · G–C = 3 H-bonds → GC-rich DNA is more thermally stable.
Explanation
The exam loves to split this two ways, so keep them straight: the phosphodiester bonds are covalent and run along a single strand, while the hydrogen bonds are weaker and hold the two strands together. That weakness is a feature — it lets helicase pry the strands apart for replication. The G–C count (three H-bonds) is why GC-rich regions resist melting, which circles back when we discuss supercoiling at high temperature later in this lesson.
Flashcard
Which bonds act within a DNA strand vs between strands — and how many H-bonds per base pair?
Within = phosphodiester (covalent backbone). Between = hydrogen bonds. A–T = 2, G–C = 3.
Q4What is the central dogma of molecular biology?
Answer
  • DNA → RNA → protein: DNA is transcribed into RNA, and RNA is translated into protein.
Explanation
This is the directional flow of genetic information and the skeleton the whole module hangs on. L2 walks each arrow in detail — transcription (DNA → RNA) and translation (RNA → protein) — and L4 shows how viruses bend the rules of this flow. Know it cold; it's the map you'll navigate everything else with.
Flashcard
State the central dogma and name the two processes.
DNA → RNA → protein via transcription then translation.
