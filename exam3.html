Ecological Terms & Diversity
M5L1 Q1What is the difference between habitat, community, ecosystem, guild, and population — and how do they rank in order of diversity?
Answer

These are nested levels of ecological organization, broadest to narrowest:

  • Ecosystem — the sum total of all organisms and abiotic factors in a particular environment; contains many habitats.
  • Habitat — the portion of an ecosystem where a community can reside.
  • Community — populations living in association with other populations.
  • Guild — a set of metabolically related populations within that community.
  • Population — microbes of the same species, same place, same time.

Order of diversity (most → least): Ecosystem > Habitat > Community > Guild > Population.

Explanation
Expect a ranking or matching item: the five terms scrambled to order by diversity, or a one-line definition to match to a term. The trap is the middle tiers — a community is all the populations together, a guild is only the metabolically-related subset (same kind of chemistry), and a population is a single species. Anchor the ends first (ecosystem = everything, including the nonliving parts; population = one species) and the middle falls into place.
Flashcard
Rank by diversity, most to least: population, ecosystem, guild, community, habitat.
Ecosystem > Habitat > Community > Guild > Population. (Ecosystem = organisms + abiotic factors; guild = metabolically related populations; population = one species.)
M5L1 Q2Contrast species richness and species abundance — how would you explain each with an example, and what does each tell you about an environment?
Answer

Two ways to express the diversity of species in an ecosystem:

  • Species richness = the total number of different species present.
  • Species abundance = the proportion of each species present (how evenly the community is distributed).

Both are functions of the nutrients available (kind and amount) and the conditions. Example: a pond with 10 species where one alga is 90% of the cells has high richness but low / uneven abundance.

Richness tells you how many kinds live there; abundance tells you which dominate and how evenly resources are shared.

Explanation
The classic stem hands you a count of species plus a lopsided proportion and asks you to characterize the environment. Don’t conflate them: a place can be rich (many species) yet have very uneven abundance (one species dominating). Richness is a headcount; abundance is the distribution. A stable, resource-varied habitat tends to support both high richness and more even abundance, so the numbers also hint at how favorable the environment is.
Flashcard
Richness vs. abundance — which is a count, which is a proportion?
Richness = number of different species (a count). Abundance = proportion / evenness of each species present.
M5L1 Q3What is a niche, and what distinguishes a prime/realized niche from a fundamental niche?
Answer
  • Niche — the habitat shared by a guild; supplies both the nutrients and the conditions needed for growth.
  • Fundamental niche — the full range of environmental conditions under which an organism can exist.
  • Realized / prime niche — the one niche in which an organism is most successful. It does not mean the organism cannot live elsewhere — just that this is where it does best.
Explanation
The distinction to nail is potential vs. actual. Fundamental = everywhere it could survive on paper (its full tolerance range); realized / prime = where it actually thrives once competition and real-world limits apply. A common stem describes an organism living across a broad range but dominating in one narrow set of conditions, then asks which term names that optimum (realized / prime). Keep niche separate from microenvironment — a niche belongs to a guild; a microenvironment is the immediate surroundings of a single cell.
Flashcard
Fundamental niche vs. realized (prime) niche?
Fundamental = the full range of conditions an organism could live in. Realized / prime = the niche where it is actually most successful.
The Microbial Environment
M5L1 Q4What is a microenvironment? Using a soil particle as an example, what types of microbes live in the different microenvironments?
Answer

Microenvironment = the immediate environmental surroundings of a microbial cell or group of cells. Its physiochemical conditions change rapidly over very small distances and short times (spatially and temporally).

Soil-particle example: a single soil aggregate holds many microenvironments. The oxygenated outer surface supports aerobes; as those cells consume O2, the interior turns anoxic, creating a niche for anaerobes. Different microbes occupy different microenvironments on the same particle.

Because resources are patchy, many microbes face a feast-or-famine existence and grow well below their lab-defined maximum rates.

Explanation
The soil aggregate is the go-to illustration of why “the environment” is never uniform: an oxygen gradient across a few millimeters lets aerobes and obligate anaerobes live on one grain at once. Expect a question asking where on a particle you would find anaerobes (the anoxic core, after surface microbes use up the oxygen). Keep microenvironment distinct from niche — it is the tiny, fast-changing surroundings of a cell, not a guild’s shared habitat.
Flashcard
On one soil particle, where are aerobes vs. anaerobes, and why?
Aerobes on the oxygenated outer surface; anaerobes in the anoxic interior, because surface microbes consume the O2 before it diffuses inward. Each is a separate microenvironment on the same particle.
Terrestrial Environments
M5L1 Q5What components make up soil, what are its four layers, and which layer is the most species-abundant?
Answer

Soil = the loose outer material of Earth’s surface. It is either mineral soil (from rock weathering / inorganic material) or organic soil (from sedimentation in bogs and marshes).

Composition: ~40% inorganic matter, ~5% organic matter, ~50% air and water, ~5% organisms.

Four layers (soil horizons): O (organic litter) → A (topsoil) → B (subsoil) → C (parent material / weathered rock). The surface / topsoil is the most species-abundant — most microbial growth is found at the soil surface.

Explanation
Memorize the four proportions — a likely stem asks for the largest component (air + water at ~50%, just ahead of inorganic mineral matter at ~40%). For the layers, the takeaway is that the top of the column is where the action is: the surface / topsoil is richest in organic matter and microbial life, and richness drops with depth. [Flag: the deck text states only “4 distinct layers” and “most microbial growth on the soil surface” — the O/A/B/C horizon names are the standard soil-science scheme; the explicitly-stated point to lock in is “surface = most abundant.”]
Flashcard
Soil is ~__% air/water, ~__% inorganic, ~__% organic, ~__% organisms — and which layer is most species-abundant?
~50% air + water, ~40% inorganic, ~5% organic, ~5% organisms. The surface / topsoil is the most species-abundant layer (most microbial growth is at the surface).
M5L1 Q6What is the benefit of having smaller cells in subsurface environments?
Answer

The deep subsurface (extending hundreds of meters down) is extremely nutrient-limited, so small cells are common.

Benefit: smaller cells have a higher surface-area-to-volume ratio, which makes nutrient uptake and waste exchange more efficient per unit of cell — a decisive advantage when nutrients are scarce.

Both Archaea and Bacteria live there in variable concentrations (set by nutrient availability); the deep subsurface is thought to host Lokiarchaeota, the archaea most closely related to eukaryotes.

Explanation
This is the Module 1 surface-area-to-volume principle resurfacing in an ecology context — the logic is identical to “why are cells small.” The answer she wants: high SA:V → more efficient scavenging of scarce nutrients in an oligotrophic environment. Watch for a distractor about storing nutrients or dividing faster; the point is exchange efficiency, not storage or speed.
Flashcard
Why are small cells favored in the nutrient-poor deep subsurface?
Smaller cells = higher surface-area-to-volume ratio = more efficient nutrient uptake / waste exchange, which wins when nutrients are extremely limited.
Aquatic & Marine Environments
M5L1 Q7What impact does water depth have on microbial species richness and abundance, and what are benthic species (where would you find them)?
Answer

Depth effect: as depth increases, light, oxygen, and nutrients all decline — light fails (no photosynthesis below the photic zone), O2 has limited solubility so deep layers go anoxic, and nutrients drop. So microbial richness and abundance generally decrease with depth; the illuminated surface water is most productive and diverse.

Benthic species = microbes attached to the bottom or sides of a lake or stream — contrast with phytoplankton, the oxygenic phototrophs freely suspended in the water. You find benthic organisms on the sediments and substrate at the bottom.

Explanation
Tie the trend to a mechanism, because that is how it is framed: deeper = less light + less oxygen + fewer nutrients = fewer, more specialized organisms. The benthic-vs-planktonic contrast is the testable pair — “attached to the bottom / sides” = benthic; “freely suspended” = phytoplankton. A stem may describe an organism’s location and ask which category it is.
Flashcard
What are benthic species, and how does depth affect richness / abundance?
Benthic = attached to the bottom / sides of a lake or stream. Greater depth → less light, O2, and nutrients → lower richness and abundance.
M5L1 Q8What are the layers associated with temperate lakes, and can you identify them?
Answer

Many temperate lakes stratify in summer into three layers (top to bottom):

  • Epilimnion — warm, less-dense surface layer.
  • Thermocline — middle layer of moderate, rapidly-changing temperature.
  • Hypolimnion — cool, dense bottom layer.

The layers differ in temperature, oxygen, and chemical composition, which supports high microbial diversity.

Explanation
Plan on an identification item — a description (“warm surface,” “cold dense bottom,” “the transitional middle”) and you pick the term. The Greek roots make it painless: epi- = upon (top), hypo- = under (bottom), thermocline = the temperature transition between them. Because each layer has its own temp / O2 / chemistry, each is effectively its own set of microenvironments — which is why diversity is high.
Flashcard
Name the three temperate-lake layers, top to bottom.
Epilimnion (warm surface) → thermocline (moderate middle) → hypolimnion (cool, dense bottom).
M5L1 Q9What is biochemical oxygen demand (BOD), what alters it, and how does eutrophication impact BOD?
Answer

Biochemical oxygen demand (BOD) = the microbial oxygen-consuming capacity of a body of water — how much O2 microbes use as they break down organic matter.

What alters it: BOD increases with an influx of organic material (waste / pollution) and decreases over time as that material is consumed. Rivers are well-mixed by flow but can still go oxygen-deficient when polluted.

Eutrophication = over-enrichment with nutrients → excess plant and algal growth. When that biomass dies, microbial decomposition consumes oxygen, so eutrophication raises BOD and can drive the water intermittently anoxic.

Explanation
The causal chain is the question: extra nutrients → more algae / plants → die-off → microbes decompose it → O2 consumed → BOD up, dissolved oxygen down, water trends anoxic. Expect a scenario (runoff, a bloom, sewage input) asking what happens to BOD and oxygen. The direction is the point — eutrophication increases BOD, and a high-BOD water is one headed toward oxygen depletion.
Flashcard
What is BOD, and does eutrophication raise or lower it?
BOD = the microbial O2-consuming capacity of water (rises with organic input). Eutrophication raises BOD — decomposing bloom biomass consumes oxygen, pushing the water toward anoxia.
M5L1 Q10What types of microbes thrive in marine waters, and which are most important for photosynthetic production?
Answer

Marine waters are high in salinity, cool, and low in nutrients (often short on nitrogen, phosphorus, and iron), yet so vast that their microbial activity is a major driver of Earth’s carbon balance.

Key marine phototrophs: Prochlorococcus, Trichodesmium (a filamentous, N2-fixing cyanobacterium), Ostreococcus (a eukaryote), plus anoxygenic phototrophs.

Most important for photosynthetic production: Prochlorococcus — responsible for ~50% of marine photosynthetic production.

Explanation
The number is the answer being fished for: Prochlorococcus ≈ 50% of marine photosynthetic production. The distractors will be the other marine names — Trichodesmium (think nitrogen fixation), Ostreococcus (a eukaryote), and especially Pelagibacter, which is a heterotroph, not a phototroph, so it cannot be the photosynthesis answer. If the stem says “photosynthetic production,” go straight to Prochlorococcus.
Flashcard
Which marine microbe accounts for ~50% of photosynthetic production?
Prochlorococcus (~50% of marine photosynthetic production). Trichodesmium fixes N2; Pelagibacter is a heterotroph, not a phototroph.
M5L1 Q11What is the pelagic zone, what organisms tell you that's where you are, and what is most abundant there?
Answer

The pelagic zone = the open waters of the ocean.

Indicator organism: Pelagibacter — the most abundant marine heterotroph and an oligotroph (grows best at very low nutrient concentrations). It carries proteorhodopsin, letting it use light energy to drive ATP synthesis. Its presence signals the nutrient-poor open ocean.

Most abundant overall: viruses — the most abundant microorganisms in the ocean (up to ~108 virions/mL), highly diverse (non-tailed dsDNA viruses dominate), and major regulators of prokaryotic populations.

Explanation
This one rewards reading the stem, because there are two “most abundant” answers depending on wording. Most abundant heterotroph → Pelagibacter (the oligotroph with proteorhodopsin). Most abundant microorganism overall → viruses. If it asks what organism tells you that you are in open pelagic water, that is Pelagibacter; if it asks what is most abundant out there, that is viruses.
Flashcard
Pelagic zone: most abundant heterotroph vs. most abundant microorganism overall?
Most abundant heterotroph = Pelagibacter (oligotroph, proteorhodopsin). Most abundant microorganism overall = viruses (up to ~108/mL).
M5L1 Q12What is the typical condition of the deep sea, and what causes variations from this?
Answer

The deep sea (~1000–6000 m) is over 75% of all ocean. Typical conditions: low temperature, high pressure, no light, and low nutrients.

Survivors must be chemotrophic (no light for phototrophy) and piezophilic / piezotolerant (pressure), and are often psychrophilic / psychrotolerant (cold).

What causes variation: hydrothermal vents — localized hot, chemical-rich oases supporting thriving communities of chemolithotrophic bacteria and thermophiles / hyperthermophiles. (In sediment, Proteobacteria dominate, then Bacteroidetes, with widespread uncultured Archaea.)

Explanation
Hold two ideas together: the default deep sea (cold, dark, high-pressure, nutrient-poor → chemotrophic piezophiles) and the exception that resolves apparent contradictions. If a stem asks how thermophiles can exist in a freezing deep ocean, the answer is hydrothermal vents — they inject heat and reduced chemicals, fueling chemolithotrophic, heat-loving communities. “No light” is why everything down there is chemotrophic rather than phototrophic.
Flashcard
Typical deep-sea conditions, and what creates the exceptions?
Default: cold, high-pressure, dark, low-nutrient → chemotrophic, piezophilic, often psychrophilic microbes. Exception: hydrothermal vents support chemolithotrophic thermophiles / hyperthermophiles.
